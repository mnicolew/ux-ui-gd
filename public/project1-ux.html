D U R A T I O N

Winter Quarter 2018 (9 weeks)

C O N C E P T

C H A L L E N G E

To redesign a pre-existing set of systems to optimize substitute’s experience booking jobs and communicating with faculty and administration.

O U T C O M E

Student, Teacher, Employee Manager (STEM) is a website that allows substitutes, faculty members, and administrators to efficiently manage employee absences.

E X P L O R E

U S E R   I N T E R V I E W S

We conducted four interviews with substitutes teachers and faculty members to determine what technological interactions (in current absence management systems) were working, or not working well. Empathizing with our user group helped us identify the needs that were not being met with existing resources. Our user research became the basis of all design decisions and later helped us create our personas and user journey map.

C O M P E T I T I V E   A N A L Y S I S

With our competitive analysis, each group member conducted research on different products/services that would potentially share the market with STEM. We focused on identifying how the product meets or fails to meet user’s goals and motivations. In completing this, we were able to use our findings to better understand our user's pain points and goals.

P E R S O N A S

Using our interview takeaways, we built two personas: a substitute (Ava) and a teacher (Malcom). More importantly, we were able to highlight our interviewee’s pain points and their goals/desires. Each persona includes characteristics, pain points, desires, goals and technology proficiency based on all of interviews to make them as realistic as possible.

U S E R   J O U R N E Y   M A P

After deciding to choose substitutes as our primary user group, we began modeling our user journey map after Ava Jones' persona. Creating the map helped us empathize and get a better understanding of what kind of emotions are associated with the pain points we identified from interviews and our competitive analysis.

D E S I G N

S T O R Y B O A R D

In creating storyboards, we were able to brainstorm our stories to put into our potential product. This helped us convey design ideas visually based on user experience and depict different aspects of our design. We chose to brainstorm representative scenarios that substitute teachers would face, such as booking a job and receiving job notifications.

I N F O R M A T I O N   A R C H I T E C T U R E

In creating our design requirements and visual storyboards, we organized our information architecture by mapping out the user's flow through the software. This helped us outline how users would interact with our product.

P A P E R   P R O T O T Y P E

We used paper prototypes to quickly visualize our three key tasks. This was an efficient way to test if our designs meet the potential user’s expectations and needs.

I T E R A T E

U S A B I L I T Y   T E S T I N G

After creating our paper prototypes, we reached out to individuals who were teachers/substitutes/students in education to receive feedback on our provisional work. Through usability testing we were able to identify our product's shortcomings and iterate on our designs before the wireframes.

W I R E F R A M E S

With our wireframes, we learned to keep it simple and focus on structure rather than functionality. Wireframing gave us an opportunity to be consistent with typography, spacing and edges.

HI-FI   M O C K U P

We created a high fidelity prototype to convey how the final product would look and operate. We were able to use our wireframes and low-fidelity mock-ups to reach this stage. Using our in class critique, we refined our design and final product to show our key tasks: booking a job, instant messaging and calendar sync feature.

R E F L E C T

P R O J E C T   R E F L E C T I O N

In terms of project takeaways, we learned user research plays a key role in design process. We practiced many research methods to stay in contact with user group, whether it’s primary or secondary. Although our user group was not easily reachable, we challenged ourselves to explore user pain points, needs and expectations. We learned to design intentionally--Designers should have reasons for each design decision, whether it’s major or minor. As we continued to progress in our project, we always remembered to reflect from our interviews and remember why we are creating an absence management system for substitutes. Additionally with more time, we all would have liked to focus more on interaction design and conduct more research through interviews or user testing. In doing so, we believe we would have added more features recommended from our user testing to better fit their goals and motivation. In conducting more user testing, we would have had more feedback on a variety of our key components. ​ More importantly, our project user group really allowed us to think outside the UW community. It has allowed us to think critically as designers and not be biased or make assumptions based on our own experience in school districts.

T E A M   R E F L E C T I O N

In the course of 9 weeks, we learned that communication is key and one of our biggest challenges as a team. From the start of our project, we struggled to find a time to meet and complete group assignments together. As weeks progress, we finally solidified the days we would meet. A typical meeting would last 3 hours the shortest up to 5+ hours. In meeting so often, we realized in order to function as group and have a cohesive goal, it was very important to update each other and hold each other accountable. This meant updating about individual progress or upcoming conflicts. However with the 9 weeks, we weren't able to fully assess our individual strengths and weaknesses. If given more time, we would have gone back as team to strengthen our project with the skills we individually thrived in. With a short time frame, we weren’t able to spend a significant amount of time figuring out the work balance with the skills we all had. We believe if we had more time to sit down and talk about our individual strengths, our project would have flowed more smoothly. ​ Overall, each member brought their own set of skills with varying backgrounds. Every decision we made was a collaborative and well thought out one. From late nights in the Sieg lounge to seeing each other at 9 AM for class, we have made many long-lasting memories together.
